Returns Across Strikes and Expirations

Examining the Impact of Delta and Expiration Selection

Introduction

Strike price and expiration date are two controllable factors on order entry when trading options. In this article we explore the historical performance of selling options at various deltas across different days to expiration in one of the most liquid option markets, SPY.

Methodology

Data is aggregated from January 2016 to December 2023 for monthly options at various deltas and different days to expiration. Each day a short option position is opened and returns recorded daily through expiration. Results are calculated for positions held through expiration and managed at various profit targets and loss thresholds. Quotes and greek caluclations are as of 3:45 PM EST each trading day with data provided by CBOE.

Results

Lower delta options had better risk-adjusted returns than higher delta counterparts. As delta increased, risk-adjusted returns decreased. Higher delta options had higher average returns, but also carried more risk. Daily P/L was consistent for each delta across days to expiration. Longer dated trades made more money but took longer to make that money.

Conclusion

A linear relationship between delta and risk-adjusted returns was observable: higher risk trades resulted in higher returns. Selling far out of the money options resulted in low overall returns with low risk. Conversely, selling closer to the money options resulted in higher overall returns but with more risk. Trade duration was not a driving factor in daily returns for positions opened between 30 and 90 days to expiration.

Considerations

This is not financial advice. Past performance is not an indicator of future results. Slippage not accounted for and trading fees not included. Risk of early exercise disregarded.
